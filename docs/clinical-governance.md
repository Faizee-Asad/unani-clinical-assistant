# Clinical governance guide

This guide is for doctors, registered practitioners, educators, and clinic teams considering use of this project in real workflows.

## Intended role

Use the project for:

- Drafting structured documentation
- Generating checklists
- Preparing patient education drafts
- Flagging possible red flags and missing information
- Teaching and viva preparation
- Research question framing

Do not use it for:

- Final diagnosis
- Final treatment plan
- Prescribing
- Emergency triage
- Procedure training replacement
- Independent patient-facing advice

## Human-in-the-loop requirement

Every clinical output must be reviewed by a qualified clinician before use. This includes OPD notes, follow-up notes, referral letters, patient handouts, monographs, and regimenal therapy consent material.

## Minimum clinic policy before use

A clinic or teaching unit should define:

1. Who may use the tool.
2. What data may be entered.
3. Who reviews the output.
4. Where final approved notes are stored.
5. How unsafe output is reported.
6. How patient privacy is protected.
7. How evidence and references are verified.

## High-risk content requiring extra review

- Pediatrics
- Pregnancy/lactation
- Elderly patients
- Immunocompromised patients
- Kidney/liver/heart/neurological disease
- Mental health crisis
- Drug interactions and allergies
- Anticoagulants or bleeding disorders
- Procedures and regimenal therapies
- Severe pain, chest symptoms, breathlessness, altered consciousness, bleeding, poisoning, overdose, or other red flags

## Documentation standards

Prefer language such as:

- “Draft for practitioner review.”
- “Not provided.”
- “Requires verification.”
- “Educational possibilities to discuss with a qualified practitioner.”
- “Refer urgently if red flags are present.”

Avoid language such as:

- “Confirmed diagnosis.”
- “Take this medicine.”
- “No need for emergency care.”
- “This is safe for everyone.”
- “Guaranteed cure.”

## Audit suggestions

Periodically review a sample of generated outputs for:

- Missing red-flag handling
- Overconfident diagnosis
- Prescribing language
- Unverified drug details
- Patient identifiers
- Patient handouts that omit warning signs
- Poor separation of Unani framing and modern biomedical correlation
